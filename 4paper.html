Making Sense of Inflation: A Beginner's Guide from Austrian Economics

When we hear the word “inflation,” we often think of prices going up and money losing its value. But according to a special way of looking at things called Austrian Economics, there's more to inflation than just rising prices. Let's break it down in simpler terms to understand what Austrian Economics teaches us.

What Inflation Means in Austrian Economics

Austrian Economics tells us that inflation isn't just about prices getting higher. It's actually about something called the "money supply" growing bigger. Think of the money supply as all the money that's floating around in an economy. When that amount increases artificially, that's when we're talking about inflation in this sense.

How More Money Can Be a Problem?

Imagine you're playing a game, and suddenly someone gives you extra points that you didn't really earn. You might start spending those points recklessly because they seem like they're worth a lot. This is a bit like what happens when there's too much money around because of inflation.

Distorted Prices and What They Mean?

Inflation messes with how prices usually work. Imagine if the price of your favorite toy suddenly went way up, not because more people want it, but because there's too much extra money in the game. This makes it hard to know what things are really worth and causes confusion.

The Up and Down Cycle

Austrian Economics also teaches us about something called the "business cycle." This is like a roller coaster ride for the economy. When there's too much extra money around, it's like the economy is on a high, and everyone feels great. But then, because things got a bit out of control, the economy crashes down like a roller coaster coming down fast.

Long-Term Effects to Keep in Mind

It might not be easy to see the problems that come with inflation right away. Imagine if you spent all your extra points on things that you can't really use or enjoy for long. That's what happens when businesses invest too much in stuff that isn't really going to help in the long run.

A Better Way with "Sound Money"

Austrian Economics suggests a different way to handle money. They talk about using something real, like gold or silver, as money. This way, there's only a set amount of money available, and it can't just keep growing artificially. It's like playing a game where you can't magically create more points — everything stays balanced.

According to Austrian Economics, inflation is defined as an increase in the supply of money and credit within an economy. This definition focuses on the monetary factors driving inflation rather than solely on changes in price levels. Austrian economists emphasize the importance of the money supply and its effects on the allocation of resources, economic coordination, and the overall structure of production.

Key points in the Austrian Economics definition of inflation include:

  1. Monetary Expansion: Austrian Economics stresses that inflation is primarily caused by the central bank increasing the money supply or by fractional reserve banking, which allows for the creation of new money through lending.
  2. Distorted Price Signals: The increased money supply leads to a distortion of price signals throughout the economy. Prices are influenced not only by changes in supply and demand for goods and services but also by the increase in money supply.
  3. Misallocation of Resources: As a result of distorted price signals, resources are allocated inefficiently, and investments are made in areas that might not be sustainable in the absence of the artificial increase in money supply.
  4. Business Cycle Theory: Austrian Economics is closely associated with the theory of the business cycle, which asserts that monetary expansions and contractions are the root causes of economic booms and busts.
  5. Long-Term Effects: Austrian economists emphasize that the negative effects of inflation might not be immediately apparent but can have long-term consequences for the economy, including malinvestments, wealth redistribution, and a potential erosion of savings value.
  6. Sound Money: Austrian Economics advocates for a sound money system based on a commodity (like gold or silver), where the money supply is limited by the availability of the underlying commodity. This would prevent the artificial expansion of the money supply and its associated negative effects.

In Conclusion

Austrian Economics gives us a new way to think about inflation. It's not just about prices going up — it's about the amount of money growing too much. This can mess up how prices work, cause economic ups and downs, and lead to problems down the road. By understanding this, we can think about better ways to manage money and keep our economy on a steadier path.
